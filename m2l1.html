Welcome to the world of PRMs

In healthcare, the patient’s voice serves as a guiding beacon for delivering optimal care.

PRMs are valid, reliable measures of patients’ health status, outcomes, and experiences of care. They are not just metrics; they are narratives that empower patients, foster empathetic dialogue, and pave the way for personalised care. Through PRMs, we can ensure that health services align with patients’ needs and expectations, elevating the quality, effectiveness, and outcomes of care.

Defining PRMs

Patient Reported Measures encompass two pivotal instruments in modern healthcare:

Patient Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs)

PROMs are defined as standardised, validated questionnaires completed by patients to measure their perception of their health status, functional wellbeing and quality of life.

Patient Reported Experience Measures (PREMs)

PREMs are short questionnaires that collect direct feedback from the patients throughout their healthcare journey. It reflects their experiences with healthcare services, providing insights into satisfaction and the quality of care.

The significance of PRMs

PRMs are valid and reliable measures of a patients’ whole person health status and they significantly complement traditional clinical measures.

PRMs are not just metrics; they are narratives that shape the healthcare landscape. They empower patients, foster empathetic dialogues, and forge a path for personalised care. Through PRMs, we strive to ensure that health services resonate with patients’ needs and expectations, ultimately elevating the quality, effectiveness and outcomes of care.

Types of Patient Reported Measures (PRMs)

Understanding the diversity of PRMs is essential as they cater to different needs and provide varied insights into a patient’s health. Here are the primary types:

Generic PRMs

General aspects of functional health and wellbeing.

They are not specific to any age, disease, or treatment.

Examples of generic PRMs PROMIS Global 10 SF-36 Health Survey

Condition specific PRMs

These are tailored to assess the impact of specific conditions on a patient's life, offering detailed insights for targeted care.

Examples of condition specific PROMs Asthma Control Test Beck Depression Inventory

Body part specific PRMs

Focused on the status and function of specific body parts, these PRMs provide information pertinent to localised health issues.

Examples of body part specific PROMs Knee, Osteoarthritis Outcome Score (KOOS) The Oswestry back disability index

The Evolution of PROMs and PREMs

This timeline offers a snapshot of the pivotal points that have shaped the use and significance of PRMs in healthcare.

Pre-1980s

Psychometrics

This new field was concerned with the construction of instruments that measured educational and psychological measurements such as intelligence tests. These questionnaires were then applied to measure people’s quality of life including their health related quality of life. The health branch was sometimes called “Clinimetrics”.

1980s

Emergence of PROMs & PREMs

Recognition of Patient Reported Outcomes begins, emphasising patient perspectives in clinical research.

1985

Health status measurements

Dr. Eddie Price, Fellow of ASLM, indicated these were not only health outcomes but health status measurements. And they required to include not only health related quality of life measures but also preventive and cared for measures.

1989

The medical outcomes study

A seminal study aimed to understand how variations in patient outcomes are influenced by different healthcare systems and clinician practices.

1990s

Proliferation of PROMs and PREMs

The development and validation of tools like the SF-36 survey mark a significant increase in the use of PROMs and PREMs.

1994

International Society for Quality of Life (ISOQOL) measures establishment

This society was established with the mission to advance the science of quality of life (QOL) and related patient centered outcomes (PCO) in health research, care and policy.

2000s

Patient centred care movement

The formalisation of patient centred care in health policy bolsters the use of PRMs to improve care quality.

2010

Michael E. Porter on Value Based Healthcare

Porter’s assertion that patient outcomes should define value in healthcare, leading to a shift from volume based to value based models.

2012

Global standardisation

Initiatives like the International Consortium for Health Outcomes Measurement (ICHOM) promote standardisation and cross cultural adaptation of PRMs.

2015

Real world evidence

Regulatory agencies begin to emphasise the importance of PRMs in generating real world evidence for healthcare decisions.

2019

Establishment of the Digital Medicine Society (DiME)

This organisation realised that health IT is the essential enabler of PRMs not only for patient assessments but also for digital therapeutics.

2020s

AI and big data

Artificial intelligence and big data analytics start to offer new ways to process and interpret large scale PRM data for personalised care.

2025

Personalised digital medicine

Platforms, such as eHealthier, incorporate PRMs and digital therapeutics as part of routine primary care and hospital practice.
