Discover the transformative approach of VBHC


VBHC is a model that prioritises health outcomes and PRM improvement and efficiency. This lesson explores VBHC‘s definition, historical evolution, and the pivotal shift from volume based to value based care, emphasising its core principles and goals.

Defining VBHC

VBHC focuses on delivering healthcare services that measure health outcomes and PRM improvements relative to the cost of delivering those services. It represents a shift from traditional volume based care (quantity) to value based care (quality).

The key principles of VBHC include patient centred care, where decisions are made based on the value the patients receive rather than the volume of services the practitioners deliver. This approach encourages healthcare systems to innovate and improve efficiency, enhancing the quality of care while controlling costs.

The significance and impact of VBHC

VBHC advocates for healthcare systems to concentrate on achieving the best possible outcomes for patients while utilising resources efficiently. The significance of VBHC lies in its potential to enhance healthcare quality, improve patient satisfaction, and ensure sustainability in healthcare systems by aligning the interests of all stakeholders towards a common goal: optimising patient health outcomes.

Through VBHC, healthcare delivery becomes more preventive, patient centric, outcome focused, and cost effective, leading to a healthier society overall.

Volume Based VS. Value Based Healthcare: A Comparative Overview

Component / aspect Volume based healthcare Value based healthcare
Focus Quantity of services provided. Patient outcomes incorporating PRM score improvement.
Payment model Providers are paid based on the amount of healthcare services they deliver. Providers are compensated based on patient health outcomes.
Goal Maximise service delivery. Improve patient health outcomes and reduce healthcare costs.
Patient engagement Limited; focus is on treatment rather than patient involvement. High; emphasises patient centred care and shared decision making.
Quality of care Inconsistent; quantity over quality. High; quality and outcome focused.
Cost implications Increased healthcare costs due to unnecessary services. Potential for reduced healthcare costs through efficiency and prevention.
Healthcare approach Reactive; focuses on treating illnesses. Proactive; focuses on wellness, prevention and improved management of chronic conditions.

The Evolution Of VBHC

This timeline offers a snapshot of the pivotal milestones that have shaped the use and significance of VBHC in healthcare.

Early 20th century

The volume based era begins

Focus on volume based healthcare, with payment models primarily rewarding quantity over quality of care.

1960s–1970s

Health Maintenance Organisations (HMOs) emerge

Emergence of HMOs aiming to control healthcare costs and improve efficiency.

1980s

Quality of care movement

Growing concern over variability in patient outcomes, marking the start of the healthcare quality movement.

1985

Quality healthcare KPI

Health status measurement (later called PRMs) identified as effective quality healthcare indicators.

2000s

VBHC conceptualisation

Introduction of VBHC by Porter and Teisberg, focusing healthcare reform on value rather than volume.

2010s

Global VBHC implementation

Global rollout of VBHC initiatives including bundled payments, ACOs, and PCMHs.

2020s

Digital transformation in VBHC

Adoption of digital health, analytics, and PROMs to enhance VBHC.

2025

Whole person wellbeing measures

Holistic healthcare integrating lifestyle medicine across physical, mental, emotional, and social health.

2030s+

AI & digital therapeutics

AI and digital therapeutics personalise care and optimise treatment in future VBHC models.
