Question design and types

Crafting questions for PRMs is a meticulous process, as the way questions are posed can greatly influence the data collected. Common types include:

Likert scales

Often used to measure the intensity or frequency of symptoms or feelings, offering a range from strong agreement to strong disagreement.

Multiple choice questions

Offer a set of predefined answers from which patients can choose, simplifying the process of quantifying diverse experiences.

Binary options

Simple yes/no responses that are straightforward to analyse and useful for clear cut questions.

Response options and their rationales

Research-validated response options are fundamental to extracting meaningful data, with the goal to:

Reflect the spectrum of experience

Ensure that patients can see their experience or situation reflected in the options provided.

Be mutually exclusive

Each option should be distinct to avoid overlap, confusion, or misinterpretations.

Be culturally relevant

Consider cultural differences that might affect how questions are understood and answered.

Weighting of responses

Weighting responses in PRMs can help prioritize certain answers over others. This is particularly useful when:

Reflecting severity

Assigning more weight to responses indicating higher severity or urgency.

Prioritising outcomes

Certain outcomes may be more critical to measure based on the purpose of the PRM.

Subscales formation and usage

PRMs often consist of an overall scale and several subscales that break down the measure into distinct domains, allowing for:

Targeted analysis

Subscales can highlight specific areas of interest or concern, such as physical health or emotional well-being.

Detailed reporting

Provides a more detailed view of patient health, which can be useful for tailored interventions.

Scoring Systems For PRMs

In the realm of PRMs, scoring systems transform raw patient feedback into quantifiable metrics. These systems must be carefully designed to ensure that the numerical scores are a reliable and accurate representation of the patient’s reported experience and outcomes. Here we delve into the components that make up these vital scoring systems.

Scoring algorithms

Scoring algorithms are the mathematical backbone of PRMs. They determine how individual responses are converted into a score that reflects the patient's health status or experience.

Simplicity vs. sophisticated

Algorithms range from simple summative scoring to sophisticated statistics such as item response theory models.

Consistency and reliability

Ensuring that the algorithm provides reliable and consistent scores across different populations and contexts.

Score mapping

Score mapping involves translating the scores from a scoring algorithm into a format that is clinically meaningful and interpretable.

Scale conversion

Transforming raw scores into standardised scales that can be easily understood and compared.

Benchmarking

Aligning scores with established benchmarks or thresholds to determine the severity or impact of patient-reported outcomes.

Score analytics and interpretation

Analytics and interpretation are where data becomes insight. This phase involves:

Trend analysis

Looking at scores over time to identify patterns or changes in a patient's condition.

Comparative analysis

Comparing scores between different patient groups or against a normative database to contextualise the results.

Predictive modelling

Using scores to predict outcomes or responses to treatment.

Norms And Goals In PRMs

In PROMs implementations, population norms derived from large-scale patient data provide essential context for interpreting individual results. These standards serve as reference points against which scores can be compared to set appropriate therapeutic targets and track progress.

Establishing norms for benchmarking

Norms are the compass that guides the interpretation of PRM scores. They provide a reference point to gauge individual patient scores against a standard derived from a larger population.

Creating normative data

Normative data is crafted from a broad patient base to benchmark individual PRM scores, offering an accurate standard for comparison and assessment.

Population specifics

Tailored norms consider various patient and clinical contexts, ensuring score interpretations are accurate and meaningful.

Usage in clinical practice

Norms help clinicians contextualise individual patient scores against expected ranges, guiding personalised care and treatment decisions.

Setting and using goals based on PRM data

Goals derived from PRM data transform abstract health concepts into concrete, actionable targets for patient care. However, these targets are only effective if there is a shared agreement between patient and provider on the goals set.

Goal setting with patients

Collaborative goal setting involves both patients and healthcare providers working together to establish achievable objectives. Providers often utilise “motivational interviewing” skills to facilitate this endeavour.

Tailoring interventions

Utilise PRM insights to customise treatments aimed at achieving the set health goals.

Monitoring progress

Employ Routine Outcome Monitoring to track advancements toward shared care goals and refine treatment strategies accordingly.
